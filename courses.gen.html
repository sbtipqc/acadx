Solving Problems Involving Exponential and Logarithmic Functions

Recall

The purpose of this section is to recall the previous lessons.

You have learned how to apply the laws and properties of logarithms, and you've learned how to solve logarithmic functions, equations, and inequalities. Also you have learned how to use these in solving Logarithmic problems. Knowing this, it can be applied in real-life applications. Now, in this lesson, you are about to learn on how to apply these in real-life situations.

Back in our past lessons, we learned about exponential functions and logarithmic Functions. But before we dive in, let us first define the term function:

A function is a relation in which each element of the domain is paired with exactly one element of the range. In other words, functions show the relation of an element to another element.

What is an Exponential Function?

Exponential functions, as its name suggests, involve exponents. But note that, an exponential function has a constant as its base and a variable as its exponent but not the other way round (if a function has a variable as the base and a constant as the exponent then it is a power function but not an exponential function). An exponential function can be in one of the following forms.

Illustration 2 | Image from Wikipedia

In mathematics, an exponential function is a function of form f(x) = ax , where x is a variable and a is a constant which is called the base of the function and it should be greater than 0.

An exponential function can be in one of the following forms:

f(x) = bx 

f(x) = abx 

f(x) = abcx 

f(x) = ex 

f(x) = ekx 

f(x) = pekx 

Where b > 0 and b ≠ 1

What is a Logarithmic Function?

Logarithmic functions are functions that are in the of the form f(x) = logax (r) y = logax, where a > 0.

Illustration 1 | Image from Wikipedia

As we discussed in the previous lessons, logarithms can be rewritten in exponential form. Recall the exponential function f(x) = bx . We shall attempt to show that its inverse is a logarithmic function.

First, recall that graphically, using the horizontal line test, it can be determined that f(x) = bx  is a one-to-one function. Hence, its inverse is also a function.

To find the inverse of an exponential function, let y = f(x) = bx ,

y = bx 

x = by  (interchange x and y)

y = logb x (change into logarithmic form)

Logarithmic functions may come in one of the following forms:

f(x) = ln(x − 2)

Examples

The purpose of this section is to help you to further visualize different topics.

Example 1: A scientist is observing a bacteria by looking through his microscope. He saw that there were about a thousand bacterias on the plate. He left the plate for a while and came back after an hour. When he checked the bacterias on the plate, he saw that it was twice as many. How much bacteria will there be on the plate if he leaves it for 4 hours if you were to use P(t) = P(2t/h  )?

Solution:

Step 1: Enumerate the given in the problem.

Formula: P(t) = P(2t/h  ); where

P(t) is the population after t hours

P is the initial population

t is the time time in hours

P(t) is the population after t hours

Step 2: Input the values in the variables. (Substitution)

P(4) = 1,000(24/1  )

Step 3: Solve for the answer.

P(4) = 1,000(24/1  )

P(4) = 1,000(24 ) (Divide 1 to 4)

P(4) = 1,000(16) (Raise 2 by the power of 4)

P(4) = 16,000 (Simplify)

Conclusion: Therefore, if the scientist leaves a thousand bacterias on a plate for 4 hours, there will be 16,000 bacterias on the plate.

Example 2: During a regular school day, the campus of the Technological Institute of the Philippines in Quezon City experienced minor shaking during class time. Even though the earthquake was not that powerful, every student was required to evacuate. Information about the earthquake was stated in the news. The energy (1010.5    J) of the earthquake was shown, but not the magnitude. What is the magnitude of the earthquake that recently occurred in T.I.P. Quezon City?

Solution:

Step 1: Enumerate the given in the problem.

Formula: M = (2/3)log(E/104.40   ); where

M is the magnitude of the earthquake

E is the energy of the earthquake

Step 2: Input the values in the variables. (Substitution)

M = (2/3)log(1010.5   /104.40   )

Step 3: Solve for the answer.

M = (2/3)log(1010.5   /104.40   )

M = (2/3)log(1010.5−4.40       ) (Apply Quotient Rule)

M = (2/3)log(106.1  ) (Subtract the exponents)

M = (2/3)(6.1) (Cancel out the logarithm)

M = 4.06666666667 ≈ 4.067 (Simplify)

Conclusion: Therefore, the strength of the earthquake that happened in T.I.P. is approximately 4.067 magnitude.

Tips

The purpose of this section is to make your learnings easier to understand.

It is important that we take these four helpful tips in mind when it comes to solving exponential and logarithmic problems:

Know the properties of exponents and logarithms

It will help you analyze the problems easier if you familiarize yourself with properties such as the product, quotient, and power rules for exponents, as well as the inverse relationship between exponential and logarithmic functions.

Be mindful

Logarithmic functions are only defined for positive arguments, so be aware of the domain restrictions when solving equations.

Always check solutions

Double check if needed. Don’t risk it and double check your answers if there is still time.

Practice

Practice is the key to mastering problem-solving involving exponential and logarithmic functions. As you work through different examples, you'll become more comfortable applying these concepts.
