Introduction to Conic Sections

Conic sections are a fascinating branch of geometry that explores the properties and relationships of curves formed by the intersection of a plane with a cone. The four main types of conic sections are the circle, ellipse, parabola, and hyperbola. Each type exhibits distinct geometric characteristics and mathematical properties.

In conics,

when ℯ = 1, then the conic is a parabola;

when ℯ < 1, then the conic is an ellipse;

when ℯ > 1, then the conic is a hyperbola;

when ℯ = 1, then the definition fails;

when ℯ < 1, then the conic is an ellipse;

when ℯ → 0, the ellipse approaches a circle as a limiting shape. The circle may therefore be considered as a special case of the ellipse.

The Parabola

The parabola is the conic section whose eccentricity is 1. In other words, it is a type of curve that is defined by a quadratic equation. It is a symmetric curve that can open either upward or downward. The standard form of a quadratic equation that represents a parabola is:
y = ax2  + bx + c. Where a, b, and c are constants. The axis of symmetry of a parabola is a vertical line that passes through the vertex of the parabola, and it is given by the equation x = −b/2a.

Illustration 2 | Image from Wikipedia

Parabolas have various applications in mathematics and physics. They are commonly used to model the trajectory of projectiles, the shape of satellite dishes, and the paths of certain types of curves in engineering and science.

Let’s recall a parabola with its vertex at (0, 0). A parabola with its vertex at (0, 0) is in the form: y = ax2 , where (a) is a constant. The general form of a quadratic equation is: y = ax2  + bx + c, but in this case, since the vertex is at (0, 0), the bx and c terms. are both zero.

Recall of Important Terminologies

The purpose of this section is to recall the previous lessons.

Direction of Opening − The "direction of opening or concavity" refers to the orientation of the parabolic curve. A parabola is a U-shaped curve that can either open upwards or downwards. The direction of the opening is determined by the coefficient of the squared term in the equation representing the parabola.

Vertex (V) − A crucial point that represents the minimum or maximum of the parabolic curve. The vertex is the point where the parabola changes direction, and it lies on the axis of symmetry of the parabola.

Focus (F) − The focus is a fixed point inside the parabola. All points on the parabola are equidistant from the focus and the directrix.

Directrix (D) − The directrix is a fixed line external to the parabola. It is a horizontal line for parabolas that open vertically and a vertical line for parabolas that open horizontally.

Latus Rectum (LR) − A line segment that passes through the focus of the parabola and is perpendicular to the axis of symmetry. The length of the latus rectum is equal to the distance between the focus and the directrix.

Endpoints of the Latus Rectum − The two points where the latus rectum intersect the parabolic curve.

Axis of Symmetry or Principal Axis − The axis of symmetry in the context of a parabola is a vertical line that passes through the vertex and divides the parabola into two symmetrical halves. Any point on one side of the axis of symmetry is equidistant to the axis as the corresponding point on the other side.

Vertex is at (0, 0)

The Value of C is the distance from the vertex to the focus and it can be obtained when you divide the coefficient to the right by 4. (+4 if the coefficient is positive and −4 if the coefficient is negative.)

Focus

When the equation is y2  = 4cx, then the formula for the focus is: (0 + c, 0).

When the equation is y2  = −4cx, then the formula for the focus is: (0 − c, 0).

When the equation is x2  = 4cy, then the formula for the focus is: (0, 0 + c).

When the equation is x2  = −4cy, then the formula for the focus is: (0, 0 − c).

Directrix

When the equation is y2  = 4cx, then the formula for the directrix is: x = 0 − c.

When the equation is y2  = −4cx, then the formula for the directrix is: x = 0 + c.

When the equation is x2  = 4cx, then the formula for the directrix is: y = 0 − c.

When the equation is x2  = −4cx, then the formula for the directrix is: y = 0 + c.

Length of the Latus Rectum

To find the length of the LR we need to multiply the value of c by 4. (4c)

Endpoints of the Latus Rectum (± 2c to focus)

When the equations are (y − k)2  = 4c(x − h) and (y − k)2  = −4c(x − h), then the formulas to find the endpoints of the latus rectum are: (Fx , Fy  + 2c) & (Fx , Fx  − 2c) .

When the equations are (x − h)2  = 4c(y − k) and (x − h)2  = −4c(y − k), then the formulas to find the endpoints of the latus rectum are: (Fx , Fy  + 2c) & (Fx , Fx  − 2c).

Axis of Symmetry or Principal Axis

When the equation is y2  = 4c and y2  = −4c, then the formula to find the axis of symmetry is: y = 0.

When the equation is y2  = 4c and y2  = −4c, then the formula to find the axis of symmetry is: x = 0.

Tips

The purpose of this section is to make your learnings easier to understand.

Certainly! Here are two tips for students when graphing a parabola at its vertex (h, k):

Focus on the Vertex Form

The vertex form of a parabola is given by y = a(x − h)2  + k, where (h, k) is the vertex. Understand that (h, k) represents the coordinates of the vertex, and 'a' affects the direction and width of the parabola.

Identify (h, k) from the equation. If it's y = a(x − h)2  + k, then (h, k) is your vertex. This is the point where the parabola reaches its minimum (for a positive a) or maximum (for a negative a) value.

Use the Vertex as a Starting Point

Plot the vertex (h, k) as your starting point on the coordinate plane.

Understand that the parabola is symmetric, so points equidistant from the vertex in one direction will have the same y-coordinate as points equidistant in the opposite direction.

Determine the direction of the parabola by looking at the sign of 'a'. If 'a' is positive, the parabola opens upwards; if 'a' is negative, it opens downwards. Use this information to plot additional points symmetrically around the vertex.
